伝 統 ・ 文 化
Living Traditions

Threads That Still Run Through Japan Today

History in Japan isn't sealed behind museum glass — these practices, born across the centuries above, are still actively lived, taught, and refined today.

神

Shinto

Japan's indigenous spiritual tradition centers on kami — spirits or sacred presences inhabiting mountains, waterfalls, ancient trees, and household hearths alike. Torii gates mark the threshold between ordinary and sacred space, and ritual purification with water remains a familiar gesture at shrine entrances across the country.

仏

Buddhism

Arriving in the sixth century, Buddhism didn't replace Shinto so much as grow alongside it, producing centuries of fruitful overlap. Today temples and shrines often sit within sight of one another, and many Japanese people move comfortably between both traditions depending on the occasion — a wedding, a funeral, a New Year's visit.

茶

Tea Ceremony

The Japanese tea ceremony, chanoyu, transforms the simple act of preparing matcha into a meditative, precisely choreographed art form. Every gesture, every placement of a bowl or whisk, is intentional, built around the philosophy of wabi-sabi — finding beauty in imperfection, simplicity, and the passing of time.

花

Ikebana

Japanese flower arranging treats negative space as seriously as the blossoms themselves. Schools like Ikenobo, with roots stretching back over five centuries, teach arrangements built around asymmetric triangles representing heaven, earth, and humanity — a quiet philosophy expressed entirely through stems and branches.

食

Washoku

Traditional Japanese cuisine is built on seasonality, balance, and umami — the fifth taste, identified by Japanese biochemistry over a century ago. From multi-course kaiseki dinners to a humble bowl of miso soup, washoku emphasizes letting an ingredient's natural character speak for itself.

着

Kimono

The kimono's elegant straight seams and wrapped silhouette have remained recognizable for over a thousand years, even as fabric, color, and pattern have evolved with the seasons and the eras. Today it's reserved largely for special occasions — but a vibrant younger generation is also reviving it as everyday street fashion.

書

Calligraphy

Shodō, the way of writing, treats brush and ink as instruments of personal expression as much as communication. The same character can look serene or explosive depending entirely on the calligrapher's brushwork — a discipline every Japanese schoolchild still practices today.

武

Martial Arts

From judo and kendo to aikido and karate, Japan's martial traditions descend from samurai combat training, refined over centuries into disciplines that emphasize control, respect, and self-mastery as much as technique. Many are now practiced — and competed in — by millions of people worldwide.

祭

Matsuri

Seasonal festivals punctuate the Japanese calendar with extraordinary energy — portable shrines called mikoshi carried through crowded streets, taiko drumming, dazzling fireworks displays, and food stalls lining temple approaches. Nearly every town and neighborhood has at least one matsuri it's proud to call its own.

庭

Traditional Gardens

From the raked gravel and stone arrangements of Zen karesansui dry gardens to the sprawling strolling gardens built around a central pond, Japanese garden design treats every viewing angle as a composed scene, often borrowing distant mountains or trees into the layout itself — a technique called shakkei.

能

Noh & Kabuki

Noh theater moves with hypnotic slowness behind carved wooden masks, prioritizing suggestion over spectacle. Kabuki, born slightly later, swung the opposite direction entirely — bold makeup, dynamic stagecraft, and larger-than-life performances that remain genuinely thrilling to watch live today.

折

Origami

The art of paper folding turns a single uncut square into birds, flowers, and intricate geometric forms using nothing but careful folds. The orizuru, or folded paper crane, has become a global symbol of peace and hope — and origami's underlying geometry now informs everything from satellite solar-panel design to medical stents.

言 葉 ・ 文 字
Kotoba

The Japanese Language

One language, three writing systems, working together in nearly every single sentence — before you meet hiragana and katakana individually further down the page, here's how the whole system fits together.

漢

Kanji

Borrowed from China starting around the fifth century, kanji are logographic characters — each one carries meaning, not just sound. Modern Japanese education centers on the 2,136 jōyō kanji, the "regular-use" characters, though most characters carry multiple readings depending on context: a native Japanese reading (kun'yomi) and one or more borrowed Chinese-derived readings (on'yomi).

三

Three Scripts, One Sentence

A single ordinary Japanese sentence routinely mixes all three scripts at once: kanji for core nouns and verb stems, hiragana for grammatical glue — verb endings, particles, connectors — and katakana for foreign loanwords, emphasis, or onomatopoeia. Far from being redundant, each script signals something different to the reader's eye at a glance.

文

Sentence Structure

Japanese builds sentences in subject-object-verb order, the reverse of English's subject-verb-object pattern, with the verb always arriving last. Small particles like wa, ga, wo, and ni attach after words to mark their grammatical role — a system that lets word order stay remarkably flexible everywhere except the very end of the sentence.

敬

Keigo — Honorific Speech

Japanese politeness is built directly into grammar, not just word choice. Entire verb conjugation systems shift depending on social context: sonkeigo elevates the listener, kenjōgo humbles the speaker, and teineigo provides a baseline polite register used with strangers, customers, and in most public-facing speech.

方

Regional Dialects

Standard Japanese, based on the Tokyo dialect, is what's taught in schools and used in national broadcasting, but regional speech remains vividly alive — from the rhythmic, comedy-friendly cadence of Kansai-ben around Osaka and Kyoto to the distinct vowel patterns of Tōhoku in the north, plus the Ryukyuan languages of Okinawa, which form their own separate branch of the language family entirely.

輸

Loanwords & Wasei-eigo

Japanese has absorbed thousands of foreign words over the centuries, reshaping their pronunciation to fit its sound system and writing them in katakana — pan (bread, from Portuguese), arubaito (part-time job, from German). Some go further still, recombining English fragments into entirely new Japanese coinages known as wasei-eigo, "Japan-made English," found nowhere in English itself.

人 物 ・ 歴 史
Faces of the Past

Figures Who Shaped Japanese History

Behind every era above stands someone whose individual choices still echo through Japanese culture today.

卑

Himiko

A shaman-queen described in third-century Chinese chronicles as ruling the Yamatai confederation, Himiko is said to have governed partly through religious authority, communicating with her people from behind ritual seclusion while a brother managed day-to-day administration. She remains one of the most tantalizing, partially mysterious figures in early Japanese history.

聖

Prince Shōtoku

Regent during the Asuka period, traditionally credited with drafting the Seventeen-Article Constitution and championing Buddhism's spread across Japan. Whether or not every achievement attributed to him reflects one person's work, "Shōtoku" became a symbol of wise, principled governance for centuries of later rulers to invoke.

紫

Murasaki Shikibu

A Heian-court lady-in-waiting who wrote The Tale of Genji in hiragana around the turn of the eleventh century, widely regarded as the world's first novel. Her psychologically rich portrait of court life remains a cornerstone of Japanese literature more than a thousand years later.

頼

Minamoto no Yoritomo

Founder of the Kamakura shogunate in 1185, Yoritomo established the template for samurai-led military government that — through various ruling houses — would shape Japanese politics for nearly seven hundred years, fundamentally altering the balance of power away from the imperial court.

利

Sen no Rikyū

The tea master most responsible for codifying wabi-cha, the austere, imperfection-embracing aesthetic at the heart of the Japanese tea ceremony. Rikyū's influence extended into architecture, garden design, and ceramics, shaping Japanese minimalist taste in ways still visible in contemporary design today.

家

Tokugawa Ieyasu

Victor of the decisive Battle of Sekigahara in 1600, Ieyasu founded the Tokugawa shogunate that would govern Japan in relative peace for roughly two and a half centuries, setting the stage for the flourishing Edo-period culture of ukiyo-e, kabuki, and widespread literacy.

北

Hokusai

The ukiyo-e master behind The Great Wave off Kanagawa, among the most recognized images in world art history. Hokusai's woodblock prints captured everyday life and dramatic landscapes alike, and his bold compositional style would later influence European Impressionist painters who encountered Japanese prints in the nineteenth century.

明

Emperor Meiji

The emperor whose name marks Japan's extraordinary late-nineteenth-century modernization, presiding over the transformation from feudal shogunate to industrializing constitutional state in barely a single generation — one of the fastest and most successful national modernizations in world history.

工 芸 ・ 技
Hand & Craft

Traditional Crafts, Still Practiced

Many of Japan's most demanding handcrafts are still taught through years of apprenticeship, passed master to student in an unbroken chain.

漆

Urushi Lacquerware

Made from the sap of the lacquer tree, applied in dozens of painstaking layers over wood or other bases, urushi lacquerware can take months to complete a single piece. The resulting surface is remarkably durable, glossy, and often inlaid with gold dust in a technique called maki-e.

陶

Regional Ceramics

From the rustic, intentionally imperfect glazes of Bizen ware to the refined porcelain of Arita, Japan's regional pottery traditions each carry centuries of distinct technique and philosophy, with many kilns still run by families tracing their craft back ten generations or more.

刀

Swordsmithing

Traditional Japanese swordsmiths fold and hammer steel through an exacting process that produces a blade combining a hard, sharp edge with a flexible, shock-absorbing spine. Master smiths today are recognized as Living National Treasures, preserving techniques refined since the medieval period.

版

Woodblock Printing

Ukiyo-e prints required a coordinated team — an artist, a block carver, and a printer — working from the same design through multiple hand-carved blocks layered in precise registration to build up each color, a collaborative craft tradition still practiced by specialist studios today.

染

Textile Dyeing

Techniques like yūzen hand-painted dyeing and shibori tie-dyeing turn plain fabric into intricate, often deeply symbolic patterns used in kimono and obi. Indigo dyeing, known as aizome, remains especially prized for its deep, durable blue achieved through natural fermentation rather than synthetic dye.

紙

Washi Paper

Handmade Japanese paper, crafted from the inner bark of plants like kōzo, is prized for its strength, texture, and longevity — durable enough to be used in shoji screens, fine art, and historical document restoration, with some washi paper remaining intact for over a thousand years.

竹

Bamboo Craft

Skilled artisans split and weave bamboo into baskets, tea utensils, and sculptural forms of remarkable structural complexity. The material's natural flexibility and strength let master craftspeople achieve curves and lattice patterns that would be difficult to reproduce in any other medium.

金

Metalwork

From temple bells to delicate kazari hair ornaments, Japanese metalworking traditions span an enormous range of scale and technique, including damascene inlay work that presses gold and silver wire into etched iron or steel to create intricate, glittering patterns.

神 話 ・ 物 語
Kamigami

Mythology: How the Islands Began

Recorded in the eighth-century Kojiki and Nihon Shoki, these creation stories still shape Shinto practice and the symbolism behind the imperial line today.

伊

Izanagi & Izanami

The primordial brother-and-sister deities credited with churning the ocean with a jeweled spear and creating the Japanese islands from the droplets that fell from its tip. Their subsequent union is said to have given birth to the many kami who populate the rest of Shinto cosmology.

天

Amaterasu

The radiant sun goddess, born from Izanagi's left eye, regarded as the most important deity in the Shinto pantheon and the mythological ancestor of the Japanese imperial line. Her shrine at Ise remains one of the most sacred sites in the country.

須

Susanoo

Amaterasu's storm-god brother, whose dramatic exploits include slaying an eight-headed serpent and discovering a legendary sword within its tail — one of the three sacred treasures still symbolically associated with the imperial throne today.

岩

The Heavenly Cave

When Amaterasu withdrew into a cave in anger, plunging the world into darkness, the other deities lured her back out with music, dancing, and a mirror reflecting her own radiance — a myth still echoed in shrine festivals featuring sacred dance, or kagura, performed today.

三

The Three Sacred Treasures

A mirror, a sword, and a curved jewel — together known as the Imperial Regalia — are said to have been passed down from Amaterasu herself, symbolically transferred at every imperial enthronement ceremony, including the most recent one marking the start of the Reiwa era.

浦

Urashima Tarō

A beloved folk tale about a fisherman who rescues a turtle and is rewarded with a visit to an underwater palace, only to return home and discover centuries have passed — a story still retold to Japanese children and echoed in countless later works of fiction.

建 造 物
Built to Last

Architecture Through the Ages

Wood, paper, and stone, arranged with a patience that has kept some of these structures standing — or being faithfully rebuilt — for well over a thousand years.

東

Tōdai-ji

Home to the Great Buddha of Nara, Tōdai-ji's main hall is, even after historical reconstructions, among the largest wooden buildings on Earth — a Nara-period engineering feat that still draws visitors marveling at both the bronze statue inside and the timber structure housing it.

姫

Himeji Castle

Nicknamed the "White Heron Castle" for its brilliant white plastered walls and elegant silhouette, Himeji is considered the finest surviving example of Japanese castle architecture, having escaped both fire and wartime destruction that claimed many of its contemporaries.

桂

Katsura Imperial Villa

A masterwork of restrained Japanese garden and architectural design in Kyoto, Katsura's teahouses and strolling garden are arranged so that the view changes with every few steps — a design philosophy that deeply influenced modern architects worldwide who studied it.

民

Minka Farmhouses

Traditional rural homes built with steep thatched roofs — engineered to shed heavy snowfall in regions like Shirakawa-gō — show how vernacular Japanese architecture adapted ingeniously to local climate using only wood, thatch, and rope, no nails required.

伊勢

Ise Grand Shrine

Japan's most sacred Shinto shrine is ceremonially rebuilt from scratch every twenty years in an ancient ritual called shikinen sengū, a tradition that has continued for over a millennium, ensuring the building techniques themselves are passed down through living practice rather than relics alone.

城

Castle Towns

Many modern Japanese cities still trace their street layouts back to the castle towns built around a daimyo's stronghold during the Sengoku and Edo periods, with moats, winding approach roads, and merchant districts that have quietly shaped urban Japan for centuries.

山 河 ・ 四 季
Fūdo

The Land Itself

Japan's geography — volcanic, mountainous, and sharply seasonal — has shaped its culture as profoundly as any emperor or shogun.

富

Mount Fuji

Japan's tallest peak and most enduring symbol, an almost perfectly symmetrical volcanic cone that has been painted, photographed, and revered as sacred for centuries, immortalized above all in Hokusai's woodblock print series Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji.

桜

Hanami & Cherry Blossoms

Each spring, cherry blossoms sweep north across the archipelago in a wave tracked nationally almost like weather forecasting. Hanami — flower viewing picnics held beneath the blooms — has been a cherished seasonal ritual since at least the Heian period.

季

The Four Seasons

Japan's distinct seasons run deep through its culture, cuisine, poetry, and even traditional dress, with an older calendar system once dividing the year into seventy-two even more finely observed "micro-seasons," each named for a small, specific change in nature.

里

Satoyama Landscapes

The traditional Japanese countryside is shaped by satoyama — a managed mosaic of woodland, rice paddy, and village existing in careful balance, a centuries-old model of sustainable land use now studied internationally as an example of harmonious rural ecology.

瀬

The Seto Inland Sea

A calm, island-studded sea separating three of Japan's main islands, historically a vital trade and travel route and today home to a celebrated art-island movement, where contemporary museums and outdoor sculpture sit alongside centuries-old fishing villages.

嶺

The Japanese Alps

A dramatic mountain range running through central Japan, nicknamed by an early Western visitor for its resemblance to the European Alps, offering some of the country's most spectacular hiking, hot springs, and traditional thatched-roof mountain villages.

沖 縄 ・ 北 海 道
Beyond the Mainland

Japan's Other Cultures

"Japan" has never meant just one culture — the archipelago's northern and southern edges carry their own distinct histories and languages.

アイヌ

Ainu Culture

The indigenous Ainu people of Hokkaido and surrounding regions developed a culture and language entirely distinct from mainland Japanese, with rich oral storytelling traditions, intricate embroidered clothing, and an animistic worldview built around respect for nature. Their cultural heritage is now actively preserved and celebrated at institutions like the Upopoy national museum.

琉

The Ryukyu Kingdom

For centuries, the Okinawan islands formed an independent Ryukyu Kingdom with its own language, royal court, and extensive maritime trade network connecting China, Japan, and Southeast Asia. Distinct Ryukyuan languages, cuisine, and traditions, including the martial art that evolved into modern karate, remain a vibrant part of Okinawan identity today.

蘭

Rangaku — Dutch Learning

Even during the Edo period's restrictions on foreign contact, a small channel stayed open through Dutch traders at Nagasaki, allowing Western medicine, science, and astronomy to filter into Japan through a field of study known as Rangaku — "Dutch learning" — that quietly prepared Japanese scholars for the rapid modernization to come.

先 駆 者
Bridging Old and New

Pioneers of Modern Japan

The postwar decades produced a generation of engineers, artists, and entrepreneurs whose work set the stage for the Japan explored in the next section.

本

Soichiro Honda

Starting from a small motorized-bicycle workshop in the rubble of postwar Japan, Honda built an engineering-driven company that would go on to become one of the world's leading automobile and motorcycle manufacturers, famous for an engineering-first culture that prized hands-on problem solving above all else.

盛

Akio Morita & Masaru Ibuka

The co-founders of Sony turned a small postwar electronics repair shop into a global consumer-technology powerhouse, pioneering the transistor radio and later the portable cassette player — compact personal electronics that quite literally changed how people around the world listened to music.

豊

Eiji Toyoda

The driving force behind the Toyota Production System, a manufacturing philosophy built on continuous incremental improvement, known as kaizen, and the elimination of waste at every step. Decades later, factories across nearly every industry worldwide still study and adapt these same principles.

手

Osamu Tezuka

Often called the "God of Manga," Tezuka pioneered narrative and visual storytelling techniques in the postwar decades that became the foundational grammar of modern manga and anime — an artistic vocabulary that the global entertainment industry explored later in this page still builds directly upon today.

文 学
Bungaku

Words on the Page

From seventeen-syllable poems to Nobel Prizes, Japanese literature has continually found new ways to compress enormous feeling into precise, careful language.

芭

Matsuo Bashō

The undisputed master of haiku, Bashō elevated a once-playful poetic form into something capable of profound stillness and insight in just seventeen syllables, famously chronicling his travels on foot across Japan in The Narrow Road to the Deep North.

近

Chikamatsu Monzaemon

Often called Japan's Shakespeare, Chikamatsu wrote extraordinarily popular plays for the bunraku puppet theater and kabuki stage, blending historical drama with deeply human stories of love and conflicting loyalty that audiences still find moving centuries later.

漱

Natsume Sōseki

Widely regarded as the most influential modern Japanese novelist, Sōseki's Meiji-era novels examined the psychological strain of a rapidly Westernizing society with a wit and interiority that helped define what a modern Japanese novel could be.

賞

Nobel Laureates in Literature

Kawabata Yasunari, honored in 1968 for prose capturing Japanese sensibility with "narrative mastery," and Ōe Kenzaburō, honored in 1994, brought Japanese literature to a global stage, both writing with a poetic precision rooted in centuries of literary tradition.

料 理
Ryōri no Rekishi

On the Plate: A Culinary History

Behind every beloved Japanese dish is a longer story — often involving centuries of refinement, and sometimes a surprising foreign origin.

寿

Sushi's Long Evolution

Sushi began centuries ago as narezushi, fish preserved by fermenting it in rice — the rice was discarded, not eaten. Edo-period innovation transformed it into fast, fresh nigiri sushi served at street stalls, the direct ancestor of the sushi enjoyed worldwide today.

麺

Ramen's Adapted Roots

Ramen traces back to Chinese wheat noodle soups introduced in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, then steadily reinvented with distinctly Japanese broths, toppings, and regional styles until it became one of the country's most beloved comfort foods.

天

Tempura's Portuguese Origin

The light, crispy batter-frying technique behind tempura arrived with Portuguese traders and missionaries in the sixteenth century, originally tied to Catholic fasting-day cooking, and was gradually refined by Japanese cooks into the delicate dish recognized worldwide today.

酒

Sake Brewing

Rice-based sake brewing dates back over a thousand years, relying on a unique mold culture called kōji to convert rice starches for fermentation — a meticulous, temperature-sensitive craft still practiced by dedicated brewers across the country.

菓

Wagashi

Traditional Japanese sweets, often built around sweetened red bean paste and seasonal motifs, are designed to be eaten alongside tea ceremony, with shapes and colors that change with the season — edible miniature artwork as much as confectionery.

郷

Regional Specialties

Nearly every region of Japan claims its own signature dish, shaped by local geography and history — Hokkaido's seafood and dairy, Osaka's beloved street food, Kyoto's refined vegetable-forward cuisine — a culinary diversity that rewards traveling well beyond any single city.

技 巧 ・ 算 術
Edo-Era Ingenuity

Traditional Science & Mechanical Wonder

Long before Japan's modern reputation for robotics and precision engineering, Edo-period thinkers were already building astonishingly intricate machines and solving advanced mathematics largely independent of the West.

機

Karakuri Ningyo

Mechanical puppets powered by wound springs and intricate gear trains could pour tea, fire arrows, or write characters with a brush — astonishingly sophisticated automata built by Edo-period craftsmen centuries before the word "robot" was ever coined, and a direct cultural ancestor of Japan's modern robotics industry.

算

Wasan — Japanese Mathematics

Developed largely in isolation from Western mathematics during the Edo period, wasan produced original solutions to complex geometric problems, often recorded on wooden tablets called sangaku hung at shrines as a kind of public mathematical offering and challenge.

図

Inō Tadataka's Map of Japan

In his fifties, former merchant Inō Tadataka walked the entire coastline of Japan on foot, surveying as he went, to produce the first remarkably accurate map of the country — a feat of patience and precision completed using little more than measured paces and astronomical observation.

暦

Traditional Astronomy & Calendar-Making

Court and temple astronomers tracked celestial movements to refine the calendar across the centuries, work that fed directly into the seventy-two micro-season system still referenced in seasonal cuisine, poetry, and festival timing throughout Japan today.

巡 礼 ・ 霊 場
Junrei

Sacred Journeys

Long-distance pilgrimage has been part of Japanese spiritual life for over a thousand years, and several historic routes remain actively walked today.

四

The Shikoku 88 Temple Pilgrimage

A roughly 1,200-kilometer circuit connecting eighty-eight temples around Shikoku island, traditionally associated with the Buddhist monk Kūkai, walked by pilgrims in distinctive white robes and wide straw hats — a journey that can take well over a month to complete on foot.

熊

Kumano Kodo

An ancient network of pilgrimage trails through the mountains of the Kii Peninsula, connecting three grand shrines and recognized by UNESCO alongside Spain's Camino de Santiago as one of only two pilgrimage routes granted World Heritage status.

高

Mount Kōya

Founded by Kūkai in the ninth century as the headquarters of Shingon Buddhism, this mountaintop temple complex remains an active monastic community today, where visitors can stay overnight in temple lodgings and join monks for morning prayers.

武 士 道
Bushidō

The Samurai World

The warrior class that governed Japan for roughly seven centuries left behind a code of conduct, a distinctive material culture, and stories that still shape how the country sees discipline and honor.

義

The Code of Bushidō

Though formally codified relatively late, in the Edo period, bushidō articulated virtues already long practiced by warrior society — rectitude, courage, benevolence, respect, honesty, honor, and loyalty — an ethical framework that still echoes through Japanese business and sporting culture today.

甲

Armor & Arms

Samurai armor, built from hundreds of small lacquered plates laced together with silk cord, balanced protection with flexibility, while the katana sword — paired with a shorter wakizashi blade — became as much a symbol of status and spirit as a weapon of war.

女

Onna-musha — Women Warriors

While samurai society is often remembered as exclusively male, women like Tomoe Gozen trained in combat and fought alongside men in several historical conflicts, wielding the naginata polearm with particular skill — a less-told but well-documented thread in samurai history.

浪

Rōnin

A samurai who had lost their master became a rōnin — a masterless warrior, a figure that became a recurring archetype in Japanese storytelling, embodying themes of loyalty lost, redemption sought, and a code of honor maintained even without a lord to serve.

暦 ・ 干 支
Toki no Dentō

Marking Time

Japan tracks time through several overlapping systems at once, each carrying its own centuries of accumulated tradition.

元

The Era Name System

Alongside the standard Western calendar, Japan continues to use gengō, named eras tied to each emperor's reign — Meiji, Taishō, Shōwa, Heisei, and now Reiwa — a tradition stretching back over a millennium, still printed on official documents, coins, and newspapers today.

辰

The Twelve-Animal Zodiac

Borrowed from China centuries ago, the twelve-animal zodiac cycle still shapes New Year's greeting cards, popular culture, and personality folklore in Japan, with each new year associated with one of twelve animals in a repeating cycle.

節

Sekku — Seasonal Festivals

Five traditional seasonal turning points, or sekku, mark the calendar year with dedicated festivals — including Children's Day in May and the Star Festival in July — each carrying its own symbolism, foods, and decorations passed down through generations.

信 仰 ・ 多 様
Shinkō

Threads of Belief

Beyond Shinto and Buddhism, several other belief traditions have left a quiet but lasting mark on Japanese history.

儒

Confucian Influence

Confucian ideas about social harmony, hierarchy, and duty arrived alongside Buddhism and shaped governance, education, and family structure for centuries, particularly during the Edo period, when Confucian scholarship formed a core part of samurai education.

隠

Kakure Kirishitan

When Christianity was banned during the Edo period, some converted communities practiced their faith in secret for over two centuries, disguising prayers and imagery within Buddhist and Shinto forms — their story is now preserved at a UNESCO World Heritage site in Nagasaki.

新

New Religious Movements

The nineteenth and twentieth centuries saw the emergence of numerous new Japanese religious movements, often blending Shinto, Buddhist, and folk elements into fresh spiritual frameworks that continue to attract followers and shape community life today.

人 形 ・ 落 語
More Stages

Bunraku & Rakugo

Beyond Noh and Kabuki, two more performance traditions have entertained Japanese audiences for centuries.

人

Bunraku Puppet Theater

Large, intricately carved puppets are brought to life on stage by visible puppeteers working in perfect coordination, accompanied by a chanted narrator and shamisen music — a deeply emotional theatrical form that Chikamatsu Monzaemon's plays helped elevate to high art.

落

Rakugo

A solo comedic storytelling art form in which a single performer, seated and using only a fan and small cloth as props, brings an entire cast of characters to life through voice and gesture alone, building to a clever punchline — a tradition still actively performed in dedicated theaters today.

遊 戯
Asobi

Games & Pastimes Through History

Long before video games, Japan had already developed some of the world's most enduringly popular strategy games and pastimes.

碁

Go

An abstract strategy board game of territorial control, played with black and white stones on a grid, that arrived from China centuries ago and was refined in Japan into a highly developed competitive and philosophical pursuit, with professional players still studied closely today.

将

Shogi

Japanese chess, distinguished by a unique rule allowing captured pieces to be "dropped" back onto the board under the capturing player's control, creating a strategic depth and pace distinctly different from Western chess, with professional shogi remaining a respected, closely followed pursuit.

歌

Karuta

A fast-paced card game built around classical poetry, where players race to grab matching cards as verses are read aloud — the competitive version, based on a thousand-year-old poetry anthology, is still played in nationally televised tournaments today.

相

Sumo's Ritual Origins

Long before becoming a professional sport, sumo wrestling began as a ritual performance offered to Shinto deities to pray for a good harvest — traces of that sacred origin remain visible today in the ring-purification rituals performed before every official match.

瓦 版 ・ 出 版
Edo Print Culture

When Books Became Popular Entertainment

Widespread literacy during the Edo period created the conditions for an entire commercial publishing industry, centuries before mass media existed anywhere else.

瓦

Kawaraban — Early News Sheets

Woodblock-printed news sheets called kawaraban circulated through Edo-period cities, reporting on disasters, scandals, and notable events for curious city dwellers — an early, informal predecessor to the newspaper, sold by street vendors who often read the headlines aloud to attract buyers.

貸

Kashihonya — Rental Libraries

Rather than buying books outright, many Edo-period readers borrowed them from traveling book lenders called kashihonya, who carried popular novels and illustrated volumes door to door — a rental library system that made popular literature accessible to a far wider audience than book ownership alone would have allowed.

紋 様 ・ 色
Iro to Kamon

Symbolism & Aesthetics

Japanese visual culture encodes meaning in remarkably specific detail — a color, a motif, or a family crest can carry centuries of accumulated association.

藍

Traditional Color Names

Classical Japanese color vocabulary includes hundreds of remarkably specific named shades — distinguishing, for instance, dozens of subtly different blues and reds — each historically tied to particular dyes, social ranks, or seasonal associations rather than simple hue alone.

松

Pine, Bamboo, and Plum

Known together as shōchikubai, pine, bamboo, and plum form one of the most enduring lucky symbol groupings in Japanese art, representing resilience, flexibility, and perseverance — pine and bamboo stay green through winter, while plum blossoms bravely while snow still lingers.

紋

Mon — Family Crests

Distinctive geometric family emblems called mon have identified samurai clans, merchant houses, and ordinary families for centuries, originally used on battlefield banners and armor, and still seen today on formal kimono and traditional storefronts.

The Crane

Said to live a thousand years, the crane appears throughout Japanese art, textiles, and folklore as a symbol of longevity, fidelity, and good fortune — the same association behind the tradition of folding a thousand paper cranes as a wish for healing or peace.

The Sound of Traditional Japan

A handful of instruments, refined over centuries, still define the distinctive sound of Japanese traditional music today.

琴

Koto

A long, thirteen-stringed zither played resting on the floor, plucked with finger picks to produce a shimmering, resonant tone — historically a refined instrument associated with court and aristocratic music, still taught and performed today.

三

Shamisen

A three-stringed lute played with a large plectrum, central to kabuki and bunraku theater music, prized for its sharp, percussive attack and expressive sliding tones that can shift instantly from delicate to dramatic.

太

Taiko Drums

Large traditional drums historically used for battlefield signaling, agricultural festivals, and religious ceremony, today performed by ensemble groups whose powerful, synchronized performances have become a genuinely thrilling live spectacle enjoyed by audiences worldwide.

尺

Shakuhachi

An end-blown bamboo flute originally used by wandering Zen Buddhist monks as a tool for meditation, capable of an extraordinarily breathy, expressive tone that ranges from haunting stillness to sudden, piercing intensity.

A Culture Built Around Bathing

Few cultures have elevated bathing into quite as central a social ritual as Japan has, across more than a thousand years of practice.

温

Onsen — Natural Hot Springs

Volcanic geology blesses Japan with thousands of natural hot spring sources, long believed to carry healing mineral properties, with bathing customs and dedicated inns built around them dating back well over a thousand years.

銭

Sentō — Public Bathhouses

Neighborhood public bathhouses became essential community gathering spaces during the Edo period, when many homes lacked private bathing facilities, fostering a tradition of communal, unhurried soaking that still continues in surviving sentō today.

風

Ofuro — The Home Bath

The Japanese home bathing ritual separates washing from soaking entirely — bathers scrub clean first, then relax in deep, hot, shared bathwater purely for comfort, a daily ritual treated as essential relaxation rather than mere hygiene.

彫

Irezumi — Traditional Tattooing

Hand-poked traditional Japanese tattooing developed elaborate full-body designs rooted in folklore, nature, and woodblock-print artistry, requiring years of patient work by specialized artists — a demanding craft tradition experiencing renewed international appreciation today.

Dōgu

Everyday Traditional Objects

Some of Japan's most distinctive traditions live not in temples or museums, but in small objects still used in ordinary daily life.

風

Furoshiki

A single square of cloth, folded and knotted in dozens of different traditional patterns, can wrap a gift, carry groceries, or bundle a bento box — a centuries-old, endlessly reusable alternative to disposable wrapping that's seeing renewed appreciation today.

弁

Bento

The compartmentalized bento box turns a packed lunch into a small, carefully composed work of art, balancing color, nutrition, and presentation — a tradition stretching back centuries that still shapes how millions of lunches are packed across Japan every single day.

暖

Noren

Split fabric curtains hung in shop doorways, called noren, signal that a business is open while offering a soft layer of privacy and climate control — often dyed with a shop's name or symbol, turning a simple curtain into a small piece of branding centuries before the concept had a name.

守

Omamori

Small embroidered charms sold at shrines and temples are believed to offer protection or good fortune for specific purposes — safe travel, academic success, healthy childbirth — carried in bags or hung from phone straps as a quiet, everyday connection to spiritual tradition.

New Year Traditions

No holiday carries more accumulated ritual than the Japanese New Year, a tradition observed with remarkable consistency across the entire country.

節

Osechi Ryōri

An elaborate New Year's meal of symbolic dishes, each layered in lacquered boxes and chosen for auspicious meaning — sweet black beans for health, herring roe for fertility — prepared in advance so families can rest from cooking during the holiday's first days.

初

Hatsumōde

Millions of people make the first shrine or temple visit of the year in the opening days of January, praying for good fortune in the months ahead — one of the most widely observed traditions in the entire country, regardless of how religious a household otherwise considers itself.

玉

Otoshidama

Children receive small decorated envelopes of money from relatives at New Year's, a cherished tradition that turns the holiday into one of the most anticipated days of the year for younger family members.

The Four Treasures of the Study

Calligraphy's tools are traditionally honored as carefully as the art itself, each refined over centuries into its own specialized craft.

筆

The Brush

Crafted from carefully selected animal hair bound around a bamboo or wood handle, a calligraphy brush can hold a remarkable range of expression in its tip — fine hairline strokes and broad, ink-saturated sweeps from the very same brush.

墨

The Ink Stick

Solid ink sticks, made from soot and animal glue and often delicately molded with decorative reliefs, are ground by hand against a wet stone to produce fresh ink — a slow, meditative ritual that's part of the calligraphy practice itself, not separate from it.

硯

A carved stone surface used to grind the ink stick with water, fine inkstones are prized as collectible art objects in their own right, their stone quality and craftsmanship directly affecting how smoothly and richly the ink develops.

Paper

Absorbent handmade washi paper completes the set, its texture chosen deliberately to interact with brush and ink in particular ways — a slightly rougher paper can make ink bloom and feather in ways a calligrapher might specifically want for a given piece.

Expo 2025 Osaka: The World Came to Yumeshima

World Expo

A Record-Setting World's Fair

Between April and October 2025, the artificial island of Yumeshima in Osaka Bay hosted Expo 2025, drawing together 158 participating nations and organizations under the theme "Designing Future Society for Our Lives." By the time the gates closed, roughly 29 million visitors had walked through — a scale that placed it among the most-attended World Expos of the modern era.

The Expo turned a profit of approximately ¥28 billion, a striking outcome for an event of this scale and a sign of just how enthusiastically both domestic and international visitors embraced it. The mascot, a swirling blue-and-red character named Myaku-Myaku, became an instantly recognizable symbol of the event, appearing everywhere from merchandise to building facades.

Far more than a trade fair, Expo 2025 functioned as a working showcase of where Japan — and its international partners — believe technology, design, and sustainability are heading next, with pavilions built specifically to let ordinary visitors interact directly with genuinely cutting-edge science.

Architecture

The Grand Ring

Towering above the Expo grounds, the Grand Ring — designed by celebrated architect Sou Fujimoto — circles the entire site for roughly two kilometers, officially recognized by Guinness World Records as the largest wooden architectural structure on the planet. Visitors could walk an elevated rooftop promenade along its full circumference, taking in panoramic views of Osaka Bay.

The structure combines traditional Japanese wooden joinery techniques with contemporary engineering, a literal physical embodiment of the Expo's broader theme: honoring craft traditions while building confidently toward the future. At night, the ring was illuminated, turning it into one of the most photographed silhouettes in Japan during the Expo's run.

Beyond its symbolic weight, the Grand Ring also served a practical purpose, providing shaded circulation and weather protection across the sprawling exhibition island, allowing visitors to move comfortably between national pavilions regardless of the Osaka summer heat.

Quantum Computing

A Quantum Computer, Open to the Public

One of the Expo's most striking exhibits let ordinary visitors interact, via tablet interface, with an actual domestically built superconducting quantum computer developed at the University of Osaka under Professor Makoto Negoro. Rather than keeping the technology behind glass, organizers designed the exhibit specifically to make an otherwise abstract, highly advanced field tangible and approachable.

The exhibit arrived against a backdrop of genuinely rapid national progress in quantum research: RIKEN, one of Japan's foremost scientific research institutes, has been developing its own superconducting quantum processors, reaching a 70-qubit milestone in 2025 — placing Japan firmly among the small group of nations pushing the frontier of practical quantum computing forward.

Quantum computing promises transformative gains in fields from drug discovery to materials science to cryptography, and Japan's decision to put a working machine directly in front of the public — rather than confining the conversation to specialists — reflects a broader national enthusiasm for bringing frontier science into everyday awareness.

Tourism Boom

A Record-Breaking Year for Visitors

2025 brought a record 42.7 million international visitors to Japan, according to the Japan National Tourism Organization — a remarkable 15.8% increase over 2024's own previous record of roughly 36.9 million. Visitor spending climbed alongside it, reaching approximately ¥9.5 trillion over the course of the year.

What makes the surge especially encouraging is its breadth: growth was spread across a wide range of source markets, including South Korea, mainland China, Taiwan, multiple European countries, the United States, and Australia, rather than depending heavily on any single region. Visitors arrived for everything from Tokyo's neon-lit districts to quiet mountain onsen towns to the cherry blossoms of Kyoto.

The Japanese government has set an ambitious target of 60 million annual visitors by 2030, paired with continued investment in transport infrastructure, multilingual signage, and regional tourism development designed to spread the benefits of tourism well beyond the handful of cities that have traditionally drawn the largest crowds.

Building Tomorrow's Tools, Today

Semiconductors

Rapidus and the Return of Advanced Chipmaking

Rapidus, a government-backed advanced semiconductor venture, has been racing to bring next-generation chip manufacturing capability back to Japanese soil at its new fabrication plant in Hokkaido. Testing of pilot production lines began in April 2025, an important milestone on the path toward commercial-scale output of cutting-edge logic chips.

The project represents one of the most ambitious industrial undertakings in Japan in decades, combining public investment with private-sector expertise to compete at the very top tier of global chip fabrication — historically dominated by a small handful of manufacturers worldwide.

Success at Rapidus would give Japan a renewed foothold in an industry it once dominated, while also strengthening the resilience of global semiconductor supply chains that power everything from smartphones to electric vehicles to the very AI systems being developed worldwide.

Materials Science

Diamond Semiconductors

Japanese researchers have achieved a world-first commercial implementation of diamond-based semiconductor technology, the product of sustained materials-science research. Diamond semiconductors can handle higher voltages, higher temperatures, and greater power density than conventional silicon, making them especially promising for next-generation power electronics.

This breakthrough has direct applications in renewable energy systems, electric vehicle power electronics, and grid infrastructure, where more efficient power conversion translates directly into less wasted energy across the system.

It's a quiet example of how patient, fundamental materials research — the kind that doesn't always make headlines — continues to give Japan an edge in highly specialized advanced manufacturing niches.

Robotics

A World Leader in Industrial Robots

Japan manufactures roughly 38% of the world's industrial robots, a staggering share that reflects decades of sustained investment in automation, precision engineering, and robotics research. From automotive assembly lines to electronics manufacturing, Japanese-built robotic arms and systems quietly power factories on every continent.

That expertise is increasingly extending beyond the factory floor. AI-guided software is now taking over delicate tasks once thought to require years of specialized human craftsmanship, while AI-paired robots are being deployed for tasks like wind turbine blade inspection and maintenance — climbing and inspecting structures that are difficult and time-consuming for human technicians to service directly.

Smart safety technology is part of this picture too: intelligent helmets equipped with sensors are being rolled out on construction and industrial sites, monitoring conditions in real time to help keep workers safer without slowing down the pace of the work itself.

Connectivity

Glass Antennas for an Autonomous Future

Japanese engineers have developed ultra-thin 5G antennas built directly into glass, designed with autonomous vehicles in mind. Rather than bulky external hardware, these antennas can be integrated seamlessly into a vehicle's windows, maintaining strong, stable connectivity without disrupting the vehicle's design or aerodynamics.

Reliable, low-latency connectivity is one of the foundational requirements for safe autonomous driving, since self-driving systems often need to communicate with surrounding infrastructure and other vehicles in real time. Innovations like this glass-antenna technology are part of the quiet infrastructure work making that future increasingly viable.

It's a fitting example of a recurring theme in Japanese engineering: solving a frontier problem — autonomous mobility — through extremely careful, almost invisible component-level design.

Finance

FINOLAB and the Fintech Wave

Tokyo's FINOLAB accelerator has become a hub for financial-technology startups, helping new ventures in payments, blockchain, and digital banking scale up with mentorship, infrastructure, and access to established financial institutions. It's part of a broader wave of fintech innovation reshaping how money moves through the Japanese economy.

Web3 technology is also being explored as a tool for regional revitalization, with several local governments and communities piloting blockchain-based projects aimed at engaging supporters, preserving local culture, and channeling new forms of investment into smaller towns and regions outside the major metropolitan centers.

Together, these efforts reflect a financial sector that's moving quickly to combine Japan's traditional strengths in precision and reliability with the speed and openness of newer digital-first approaches.

Space Exploration

JAXA: Reaching Past the Sky

Asteroid Missions

Hayabusa & Hayabusa2

Japan's space agency, JAXA, pulled off one of the great feats of robotic exploration with its Hayabusa missions. The original Hayabusa probe returned to Earth in 2010 carrying microscopic samples from asteroid Itokawa — the first time any spacecraft had brought material back from an asteroid surface.

Its successor, Hayabusa2, launched in 2014, traveled to the carbon-rich asteroid Ryugu, deployed small rovers onto its surface, fired a projectile to expose subsurface material, and successfully returned samples to Earth in December 2020 — a mission widely regarded as a model of precision deep-space engineering.

Those Ryugu samples are still being studied by researchers around the world, offering clues about the early solar system and the organic chemistry that may have helped seed the ingredients for life on Earth billions of years ago.

Lunar & Rocket Programs

SLIM and the H3 Rocket

JAXA's SLIM lander — nicknamed the "Moon Sniper" for its unprecedented pinpoint landing accuracy — successfully touched down on the lunar surface, demonstrating precision landing technology that could prove essential for future lunar exploration and eventual sustained presence on the Moon.

Supporting these ambitions is the H3 rocket, Japan's next-generation launch vehicle, developed to carry satellites, cargo, and future missions into space more efficiently and at lower cost than its predecessors, launching from the Tanegashima Space Center off the southern coast of Japan.

Together, these programs are steadily building the launch and landing infrastructure Japan needs to support an increasingly ambitious roster of missions throughout the solar system.

Mars Moons

MMX: A Sample-Return Mission to Phobos

JAXA's Martian Moons eXploration mission, planned for launch around fiscal year 2026, aims to do for the moons of Mars what Hayabusa2 did for Ryugu: travel to Phobos, collect a physical sample, and return it safely to Earth for detailed laboratory study.

Scientists hope samples from Phobos will help settle one of the more intriguing open questions in planetary science — whether Mars's small, oddly shaped moons are captured asteroids or debris from an ancient impact on Mars itself.

The mission builds directly on the sample-return expertise JAXA has refined over two successful asteroid missions, extending Japan's deep-space reach significantly further out into the solar system.

International Space Station

Robots Working Together in Orbit

In July 2025, JAXA's free-flying assistant robot Int-Ball2 and the European Space Agency-affiliated CIMON robot achieved the first successful robot-to-robot collaboration aboard the International Space Station — a small but genuinely historic milestone in how robotic systems from different space agencies can work together in orbit.

This took place aboard Kibo, Japan's own ISS laboratory module, which has hosted a steady stream of Japanese-led microgravity research since it became operational. Japanese astronauts including Onishi Takuya and Yui Kimiya rotated through ISS missions in 2025, continuing Japan's long, active role in crewed spaceflight.

From Akatsuki's ongoing study of Venus's atmosphere to the earlier Kaguya lunar orbiter mission and the experimental IKAROS solar-sail spacecraft, JAXA has built an exploration portfolio spanning nearly every part of the solar system within reach.

International Collaboration

BepiColombo: A Joint Journey to Mercury

JAXA is also a key partner, alongside the European Space Agency, in the BepiColombo mission to Mercury — a joint effort combining a European orbiter and a Japanese-built orbiter to study the solar system's innermost planet in unprecedented detail once both spacecraft reach Mercury orbit.

International collaborations like this reflect Japan's role as a trusted, technically capable partner in the global space community, contributing specialized engineering expertise to missions whose scientific ambitions exceed what any single space agency could practically accomplish alone.

Combined with its own solo missions, Japan's collaborative space efforts illustrate a deep, sustained national investment in pushing the boundaries of what's knowable about our solar system.

Aviation

Shark-Skin Coatings for Cleaner Flight

In a joint project between JAXA and Japan Airlines, engineers have trialed riblet film coatings — inspired by the microscopic texture of shark skin — applied to aircraft surfaces to reduce aerodynamic drag during flight.

Early trials suggest the coating can meaningfully cut fuel consumption and associated carbon dioxide emissions per flight, a promising, relatively low-cost retrofit technology that could be applied across existing commercial aircraft fleets rather than requiring entirely new plane designs.

It's a clean example of biomimicry — engineering inspired directly by nature's own aerodynamic solutions — being put to practical, fuel-saving use in commercial aviation.

Engineering a Greener Tomorrow

Materials

Plastic Made From Captured Carbon

Japanese chemical manufacturers have developed biodegradable plastic produced using captured carbon dioxide as a feedstock, turning a greenhouse gas that would otherwise contribute to warming into a useful industrial material. The resulting plastic breaks down naturally at the end of its life, rather than persisting in the environment for centuries.

This kind of carbon-recycling approach represents a meaningfully different strategy than simply reducing emissions — it actively repurposes captured carbon into products people already use every day, closing part of the loop between industrial output and environmental impact.

As the technology scales, it offers a template other industries could adapt: treating captured carbon not as a problem to be buried, but as a raw material with genuine commercial value.

Water & Energy

A Desalination Plant That Also Powers Itself

A desalination facility in Fukuoka has been engineered to generate electricity as part of its own operation, turning what's traditionally a heavily energy-consuming process into a far more self-sufficient one. Clean drinking water and renewable power emerge from the same integrated system.

Facilities like this matter enormously for regions facing water scarcity, since traditional desalination's high energy demands have historically limited where it makes economic and environmental sense to deploy. A self-powering design substantially changes that calculus.

It's a strong example of systems-level engineering thinking — rather than treating water and energy as separate problems, the Fukuoka plant treats them as one integrated design challenge, with each part of the system supporting the other.

Circular Economy

Power From Vegetable Scraps

Japanese researchers and municipalities have developed methane-generation systems that convert weeds and discarded vegetable scraps into usable power, turning organic waste that would otherwise be discarded into a genuine, renewable energy source for local communities.

These small-scale biogas systems are especially valuable in agricultural regions, where they give farms and food-processing operations a practical way to handle organic waste streams while generating energy on-site rather than depending entirely on the broader grid.

It's a quiet but meaningful example of circular-economy thinking in action: closing the loop between food production, food waste, and energy generation within the same local system.

Ocean Conservation

Collecting Microplastics at Sea

A specially engineered cargo-ship attachment has been developed to collect ocean microplastics passively while vessels go about their normal shipping routes, turning routine maritime traffic into an active tool for ocean cleanup rather than adding additional dedicated cleanup voyages.

By integrating collection directly into existing shipping operations, the approach can cover vastly more ocean distance than any standalone cleanup vessel could manage on its own, while imposing minimal disruption to commercial shipping schedules.

Innovations like this reflect a broader Japanese engineering instinct: look for ways to fold environmental benefit directly into systems that already exist, rather than building entirely separate, costlier infrastructure from scratch.

Sustainable Materials

Silk From Bagworms

Researchers have identified bagworm silk — produced by a small moth caterpillar known for building protective cases from silk and debris — as an unusually strong, sustainable fiber source, with mechanical properties that make it an intriguing candidate for next-generation sustainable textiles.

Unlike conventional silkworm farming, bagworm-derived fiber research points toward production methods that could require fewer resources, opening the door to genuinely sustainable luxury and technical textiles grown from a previously overlooked insect.

It's part of a broader pattern in Japanese materials science: looking to the natural world's own engineering — spun fibers, shark skin, diamond crystal structure — for solutions that outperform purely synthetic alternatives.

Aquaculture

AI-Guided Fish Farming

Japanese aquaculture operations are increasingly using AI systems to monitor water quality, feeding patterns, and fish health in real time, allowing farms to optimize conditions continuously rather than relying solely on periodic manual checks.

More efficient aquaculture matters directly for food security and ocean conservation alike, since well-managed fish farms can ease pressure on wild fish stocks while still meeting the country's substantial demand for fresh seafood.

Combined with precision feeding systems that reduce waste, AI-guided aquaculture represents exactly the kind of unglamorous, highly practical innovation that quietly improves both sustainability and food quality at the same time.

Innovation You Can Feel

Regenerative Medicine

"Tetra-Gel" and the Future of Healing

Japanese biomedical researchers have developed an advanced regenerative material, sometimes referred to as "tetra-gel," engineered with a uniquely uniform molecular network structure that gives it remarkable strength and elasticity for a hydrogel-based material.

Materials like this hold real promise for regenerative medicine applications — from cartilage repair to soft-tissue reconstruction — precisely because their structural reliability at a microscopic level translates into more predictable, durable performance once implanted in the body.

It's research at the genuine cutting edge of biomaterials science, the kind of fundamental advance that often takes years to reach clinical use but can ultimately transform recovery and quality of life for patients once it does.

Prosthetics

A Bionic Hand With Natural Motion

An Osaka-based startup unveiled a bionic prosthetic hand at Expo 2025 capable of reproducing roughly 95% of natural hand motion, a remarkable benchmark for dexterity in a prosthetic device, demonstrated live to Expo visitors throughout the event's run.

Prosthetics at this level of articulation can dramatically expand what's possible for users in daily life — from fine motor tasks like typing or holding utensils to more demanding physical activities that older-generation prosthetics simply couldn't support comfortably.

Showcasing the device directly at Expo, rather than only within medical or academic settings, let ordinary visitors see firsthand just how far prosthetic technology has advanced in recent years.

Energy & Mobility

Hydrogen on the Road

Toyota's Mirai, one of the world's most prominent hydrogen fuel-cell vehicles, continues to anchor Japan's broader push toward a "hydrogen society" — an energy vision in which hydrogen plays a central role alongside batteries in powering everything from passenger cars to buses to industrial equipment.

Hydrogen fuel-cell vehicles refuel in minutes rather than charging for hours, and emit only water vapor from the tailpipe, offering a complementary path to clean transportation alongside battery-electric vehicles, particularly for longer-range and heavier-duty use cases.

Continued investment in hydrogen refueling infrastructure across Japan reflects a genuinely long-term national bet that a diversified clean-energy transportation system — rather than a single dominant technology — will serve the country best.

A Culture the Whole World Has Embraced

Anime & Manga

A Global Storytelling Powerhouse

Japanese anime and manga have grown from a domestic entertainment industry into one of the most influential storytelling exports on Earth, with dedicated fan communities, conventions, and streaming audiences spanning essentially every country with internet access. Long-running franchises sit comfortably alongside an endless stream of fresh, original series each season.

Manga's influence runs equally deep on the printed page, with bookstores worldwide now dedicating substantial shelf space to translated volumes, while original Japanese art and storytelling conventions have visibly shaped comics and animation produced well outside Japan itself.

What makes this cultural export especially remarkable is its sheer range — from intimate slice-of-life drama to sprawling fantasy epics — giving it a genuine claim to being one of the most versatile and widely loved storytelling mediums in the world today.

Video Games

Defining an Entire Medium

Japanese studios and console makers have shaped the video game industry from its earliest days, and that influence remains as strong as ever, with Japanese-developed franchises consistently ranking among the best-selling and most critically celebrated games released anywhere in the world each year.

Beyond individual titles, Japanese design philosophy — meticulous attention to feel, pacing, and player experience — has become something of an industry-wide benchmark, studied and referenced by developers worldwide regardless of where their own studios are based.

From handheld consoles to globally streamed esports tournaments, Japan's gaming industry continues to evolve while staying remarkably central to how the medium as a whole keeps growing.

Cuisine

Tokyo, World Capital of Michelin Stars

Tokyo has held the distinction of being the city with the most Michelin-starred restaurants anywhere in the world for years running, an extraordinary concentration of culinary excellence spanning everything from intimate sushi counters seating barely a handful of guests to elaborate kaiseki tasting menus.

Beyond fine dining, Japanese food culture has reshaped how the rest of the world eats casually too — ramen shops, sushi restaurants, and izakaya-style dining have become fixtures of major cities on every continent, introducing global audiences to flavors and techniques refined in Japan over centuries.

This culinary influence flows in both directions: Japanese chefs continue to draw inspiration from global ingredients and techniques, even as washoku itself was formally recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage, celebrated for its emphasis on seasonality, balance, and respect for ingredients.

Rail Technology

The Next Generation of Shinkansen

More than sixty years after the original bullet train debuted alongside the 1964 Tokyo Olympics, Japan's Shinkansen network remains a global byword for high-speed rail done right — punctual to the second, remarkably smooth, and continuously upgraded with newer, faster, more energy-efficient train sets.

Looking further ahead, the Chuo Shinkansen maglev line is under development to connect Tokyo and Osaka using magnetic levitation technology capable of speeds exceeding 500 kilometers per hour, floating just above the track rather than relying on traditional wheels and rails.

Six decades after reinventing what train travel could be, Japan is once again pushing the technology forward — a fitting symbol of a country that treats its proudest achievements not as finished monuments, but as platforms to keep building on.

A Design Philosophy the World Keeps Borrowing

Architecture

Concrete, Light, and Quiet

Architects like Tadao Ando have made bare concrete, carefully placed light, and deliberate empty space into a globally celebrated design language, with museums, chapels, and private homes around the world now drawing directly on principles refined first in Japanese architectural practice.

A younger generation, including Kengo Kuma, has pushed this further by reintroducing natural materials — wood, stone, woven screens — into contemporary buildings, softening the line between traditional craft and modern construction technique in landmark projects across Japan and internationally.

This design sensibility extends well past famous landmarks: it's visible in train stations, convenience stores, and ordinary apartment buildings, where careful proportion and restraint are treated as standard practice rather than luxury.

Product Design

"No Brand" as a Global Brand

Muji's philosophy of stripped-down, unbranded simplicity — products designed around function and material honesty rather than logos or ornamentation — has become one of Japan's most widely recognized design exports, with stores now operating across dozens of countries worldwide.

This minimalist sensibility runs through Japanese product design more broadly: stationery, kitchenware, and electronics frequently prioritize thoughtful ergonomics and quiet visual restraint, an aesthetic that's increasingly influential well beyond Japan's own borders.

The underlying idea — that good design should feel inevitable rather than attention-seeking — has become something of a global design-school touchstone, regularly cited by designers working in entirely unrelated industries and countries.

Urban Planning

Cities Built for Walking and Trains

Japanese cities are frequently held up internationally as models of dense, walkable, transit-oriented urban design, with extensive train and subway networks that make car ownership genuinely optional for most residents of major metropolitan areas.

This urban model supports vibrant neighborhood-level commerce — small shops, restaurants, and services clustered around train stations — creating the kind of dense, lively street life that urban planners elsewhere actively study and try to replicate.

The combination of safety, cleanliness, and convenience found in major Japanese cities consistently ranks them among the most livable urban environments in global surveys, a track record that continues to draw planners and policymakers from around the world for study visits.

Seismic Engineering

Skyscrapers That Sway, On Purpose

Decades of research into earthquake engineering have given Japan some of the most advanced seismic-resistant building technology anywhere in the world, including base-isolation systems that let entire buildings shift gently on specialized bearings, absorbing ground motion before it ever reaches the structure above.

Tuned mass dampers — massive counterweights suspended inside skyscrapers — work to cancel out swaying motion during both earthquakes and strong winds, technology now closely studied and adapted by structural engineers designing tall buildings in seismically active regions worldwide.

This expertise has turned into an internationally sought-after export of its own, with Japanese seismic engineering knowledge and technology now incorporated into major building projects across multiple countries facing similar earthquake risks.

Early Warning Systems

Seconds That Save Lives

Japan operates one of the most sophisticated earthquake early-warning networks in the world, capable of detecting the first, faster-traveling seismic waves and broadcasting alerts to phones, trains, and factories in the brief window before the more damaging shaking arrives.

Those precious seconds are enough to automatically slow high-speed trains, halt elevators at the nearest floor, and give people time to take cover — a quietly remarkable feat of distributed sensor engineering operating continuously, nationwide, every single day.

The underlying detection and rapid-alert technology continues to be refined further, with researchers working to shrink the warning delay even more and extend coverage to increasingly remote areas of the country.

Combined with the country's nuclear and hydrogen research programs, this diversified renewable strategy reflects a clear long-term commitment: building an energy system resilient enough to support Japan's technological ambitions for decades to come.

Flood Control

The Metropolitan Area Outer Underground Discharge Channel

Beneath the suburbs of greater Tokyo lies one of the largest underground flood-control systems on Earth — a network of massive vertical shafts connected by a colossal underground tank, supported by towering concrete pillars, that diverts excess floodwater away from vulnerable neighborhoods during heavy storms.

The Same Patience, Pointed Forward

Looking Forward

A Country That Keeps Building

Read end to end, what's striking about today's Japan isn't any single achievement — it's the sheer breadth of fronts on which careful, patient effort is paying off simultaneously: orbit, ocean floor, hospital ward, rice paddy, and city street alike.

None of it happened by accident. Decades of investment in education, infrastructure, and research culture set the stage, and a cultural comfort with incremental refinement — kaizen, monozukuri, the same instinct that perfected lacquerware over centuries — kept it moving forward year after year.

Two scripts are still waiting below, each one a reminder that this same instinct for patient refinement reaches all the way back to a quiet innovation in a Heian-era court more than a thousand years ago — and it's still very much in motion today.

ひらがな

Hiragana

The soft, rounded script born in the Heian court — click any character below to watch its stroke order, learn a mnemonic, and meet the kanji it descended from.

あ

Hiragana grew out of man'yōgana, the Nara-period system of using Chinese characters purely for their sound. Heian court scribes — and especially women, who were largely excluded from formal Chinese scholarship — gradually simplified those characters into fast, flowing cursive shapes. The result earned the nickname onna-de, "women's hand," and within a generation it had produced The Tale of Genji, widely considered the world's first novel.

Every hiragana character today traces back to one specific man'yōgana kanji, abbreviated through centuries of handwriting into the rounded, flowing forty-six-character set still taught to every Japanese schoolchild. It wasn't formally standardized until 1900 — before that, multiple cursive variants for the same sound, called hentaigana, coexisted and were used somewhat interchangeably, some of which still appear today on traditional shop signs and soba-noodle restaurant noren.

Native Japanese grammar — verb endings, particles, and any native word without an assigned kanji.

Beyond the Basic 46

Two small marks and a handful of combination characters extend hiragana to cover every sound in Japanese. A dakuten (゛) added to certain consonants voices the sound — か (ka) becomes が (ga). A handakuten (゜), used only on the h-row, turns h into a crisp p sound — は (ha) becomes ぱ (pa).

カタカナ

Katakana

Sharp, angular, and built for clarity — click any character below to watch its stroke order, learn a mnemonic, and meet the kanji it descended from.

ア

Katakana has a very different birthplace than hiragana. Buddhist monks studying Chinese scripture needed a fast way to mark Japanese pronunciation and grammar notes directly in the margins of dense classical texts — there wasn't room for full cursive script, so they abbreviated man'yōgana kanji down to small angular fragments, often just a single corner or radical of the original character. That clipped, efficient shorthand became katakana.

Because of its origins in formal religious and scholarly annotation, katakana carried a more official, serious connotation for centuries, while hiragana was associated with personal and literary writing. That hierarchy has completely flipped in modern usage: today katakana's most common job is spelling out loanwords from other languages, scientific and technical terms, onomatopoeia, and anything a writer wants to set apart for emphasis — doing for Japanese roughly what italics do in English.
